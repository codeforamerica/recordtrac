Equally important as the number of requests is who is submitting the requests. A number of staff members vocalized their concern that attorneys were using the system as free discovery. To test this theory, we looked at the types of organizations requesting public records. The majority of individuals did not identify themselves as a part of a company or organization. Of those that did identify themselves, 118 requests came from lawyers and 97 from members of the media.

A field in the raw data suggest there is a way for the user to flag the City Attorney’s office in case a document needs to be reviewed. This has only been done once.

It was difficult to neatly categorize the requests in tidy categories. That being said, some of the most popular documents requested are police reports, building records (liens, permits, etc.), and documents about the city's finances. Financial documents include the city's budget, employee salaries, and information about individuals and organizations contracting with the city.

Certain topics attracted the attention of the public as well. They include the redevelopment of the Oakland Army Base, Occupy Oakland, medical marijuana dispensaries, and all activities taken by the Oakland Police Department.

We also observed the residents often use the public records request system as a way to get information from the city or submit complaints. Sample inquiries include how to dissolve domestic partnership and get a record expunged. Residents often ask the city for documents or records it does not keep, like birth certificates.

Based on our analysis of the data extracted, we discovered:

  • The current system captures requests made through the city's website.
  • Most public records requests are funnelled to the city clerk or police services.
  • To reduce the number of requests, the city should actively release documents that would be of interest to the public. This could include information about the budget or payments to contractors.
